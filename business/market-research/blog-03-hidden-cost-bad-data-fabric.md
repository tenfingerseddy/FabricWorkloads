# The Hidden Cost of Bad Data in Fabric: How Data Quality Issues Silently Drain Your CU Budget

## Introduction

Every Microsoft Fabric team knows about the visible costs: your capacity SKU, the monthly Azure bill, the occasional burst charge when a Spark job runs hot. These are the costs you plan for. They appear on spreadsheets. People approve them.

But there is another category of cost that never appears in any budget: the CU consumption generated by data quality failures. Failed pipelines that retry. Notebooks that reprocess entire datasets because upstream data arrived late or corrupted. Semantic model refreshes that run on stale data, triggering manual re-runs when a stakeholder notices. Dataflows that silently error and kick off cascading downstream re-executions.

This is the tax your organization pays for not having observability. And based on our analysis of real Fabric workspaces, it is significantly larger than most teams estimate.

## How Fabric CU Billing Actually Works (The Short Version)

Before we calculate waste, we need to understand the billing model. Fabric uses Capacity Units (CUs) as a universal compute currency. Every operation -- a pipeline run, a notebook execution, a Spark query, a semantic model refresh, a Dataflow Gen2 transformation -- consumes CUs proportional to the compute resources it uses and the time it runs.

Here are the key mechanics:

**Per-second billing.** You are charged for actual compute consumed, measured in CU-seconds. A notebook that runs for 300 seconds on 8 CUs consumes 2,400 CU-seconds. There is no rounding up to the next hour or next minute.

**Smoothing.** Fabric smooths CU consumption over time windows (typically 5 minutes for interactive and 24 hours for background). This means a burst of activity does not immediately trigger throttling, but sustained over-consumption will. Background smoothing is particularly relevant because most data engineering workloads -- pipelines, notebooks, dataflows -- run as background operations with a 24-hour smoothing window.

**Throttling and rejection.** When cumulative consumption exceeds your capacity's available CUs over the smoothing window, Fabric first delays (throttles) background operations, then rejects new submissions entirely. This is when failures cascade: throttled jobs timeout, timeout triggers retries, retries consume more CUs, and the cycle accelerates.

**Bursting.** Fabric allows temporary bursting above your SKU's nominal CU count, borrowing from future capacity. This keeps things running during spikes but creates a "debt" that must be repaid in subsequent time windows. If the debt is not repaid, throttling kicks in.

The critical insight is this: **wasted CU consumption does not just waste money -- it steals capacity from legitimate workloads.** A pipeline that unnecessarily retries three times does not just cost 3x; it can push your workspace into throttling, which then impacts every other workload on the same capacity.

## The Five Categories of CU Waste

We have identified five distinct categories of CU waste caused by data quality and observability gaps. Each one is preventable with proper monitoring.

### 1. Retry Waste: The Failed Pipeline Loop

This is the most obvious category. A pipeline fails, Fabric retries it (if retry policies are configured), or an engineer manually re-runs it. Each attempt consumes CUs regardless of outcome.

**The math:**

Consider a daily data pipeline on an F64 capacity (64 CUs at $10.99/hour in pay-as-you-go):

| Scenario | Runs/Day | Avg Duration | CU Consumption | Monthly CU Cost |
|----------|----------|-------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Healthy (no failures) | 1 | 45 minutes | 2,880 CU-seconds | ~$62 |
| 15% failure rate + 1 retry | 1.15 | 45 min each | 3,312 CU-seconds | ~$71 |
| 15% failure rate + 2 retries | 1.30 | 45 min each | 3,744 CU-seconds | ~$81 |
| 15% failure + cascading downstream | 1.30 + downstream | 45 min + 30 min downstream | 5,544 CU-seconds | ~$120 |

A single pipeline with a 15% failure rate and cascade effects costs nearly **2x its expected cost**. Multiply across 20 pipelines in a workspace and you are looking at hundreds of dollars per month in pure waste -- on a single capacity.

But the real cost is not the dollars. It is the capacity consumption. Those wasted CU-seconds push your workspace closer to throttling thresholds, which degrades performance for every user and every workload on the capacity.

### 2. Stale Data Reprocessing: The "Run It Again" Tax

This is the category teams rarely measure. When a report shows stale data because an upstream failure went undetected, someone eventually notices (usually a stakeholder, not an alert). The response is almost always the same: manually trigger the pipeline, the notebook, and the semantic model refresh. This ad-hoc re-execution consumes CUs that would not have been needed if the original failure had been caught and fixed promptly.

**How it compounds:**

1. Pipeline fails silently at 2 AM (CU consumed: wasted)
2. Downstream notebook runs on stale data (CU consumed: wasted, because the output is wrong)
3. Semantic model refreshes from stale Lakehouse (CU consumed: wasted)
4. Stakeholder notices at 10 AM, engineer manually re-runs the pipeline (CU consumed: necessary but duplicated)
5. Notebook re-runs on fresh data (CU consumed: necessary but duplicated)
6. Semantic model refreshes again (CU consumed: necessary but duplicated)

In this scenario, steps 1-3 consumed CUs producing wrong outputs. Steps 4-6 are the legitimate execution. You paid double. And because steps 4-6 are ad-hoc, they compete with scheduled workloads for capacity, potentially triggering throttling during business hours.

### 3. Schema Drift Cascades: The Destructive Rebuild

Fabric Dataflow Gen2 has a known behavior when upstream schemas change: in some configurations, the destination table is dropped and recreated. When this happens, any relationships, measures, or calculated columns added to that table in the Lakehouse are lost. Teams then need to manually rebuild and re-run the pipeline.

The CU cost here is not just the re-execution -- it is the hours of engineering time, plus the downstream refresh failures that pile up while the schema is broken. Each failed refresh attempt consumes CUs and generates error logs that nobody can easily search because the Monitoring Hub only holds 30 days and 100 visible activities.

### 4. Timeout-Induced Full Reprocessing

OAuth tokens expire after approximately 1 hour. If a data refresh takes longer than the token lifetime, it can fail with a credential error. Scheduled refreshes for imported semantic models timeout after 2 hours (5 hours in Premium workspaces). When these timeouts hit, the entire refresh starts over from scratch.

For large datasets, this is devastating. Consider a semantic model refresh that processes 500 million rows:

- Attempt 1: Runs for 1 hour 50 minutes, times out at 2 hours. **All CUs consumed, zero useful output.**
- Attempt 2: Starts over, runs for 1 hour 50 minutes, times out again. **All CUs consumed again.**
- Engineer intervenes, enables incremental refresh, attempt 3 succeeds in 20 minutes.

The first two attempts consumed nearly 4 hours of full-capacity CUs with zero value delivered. With proper observability -- duration trending, timeout predictions, proactive alerts -- the engineer would have caught the creeping duration increase weeks earlier and implemented incremental refresh before the first timeout.

### 5. Duplicate Scheduled Runs: The Configuration Drift Problem

In workspaces with multiple pipelines, notebooks, and dataflows, schedule configurations drift over time. Common patterns:

- Two pipelines refresh the same semantic model (one scheduled, one triggered by a pipeline activity). The model refreshes twice, every time.
- A notebook has both a workspace schedule and a pipeline trigger. It runs twice.
- A Dataflow Gen2 is triggered by a pipeline AND has its own schedule. Double execution.

Each duplicate execution consumes CUs for work that produces identical results. Without a unified view of all schedules and trigger sources across the workspace, these duplicates are invisible. The Monitoring Hub shows each run as a separate, successful execution -- because it is. It just should not have happened.

## Calculating Your Workspace's CU Waste

Here is a practical approach to estimate the CU waste in your Fabric workspace. You can run this analysis today using the Fabric REST APIs.

### Step 1: Collect Job Failure Data

Use the Jobs API to pull job instances for every item in your workspace:

```http
GET https://api.fabric.microsoft.com/v1/workspaces/{workspaceId}/items/{itemId}/jobs/instances
```

Calculate your failure rate per item over the last 30 days:

```
Failure Rate = Failed Instances / Total Instances
```

Any item with a failure rate above 5% is a significant CU waste source.

### Step 2: Identify Retry Patterns

Look for job instances that start within minutes of a failed instance for the same item. These are retries -- either automatic or manual. Each retry is a CU cost that would not exist if the root cause had been addressed.

### Step 3: Find Duplicate Schedules

Query the scheduled runs for each item and look for overlapping execution windows. If a notebook runs at 2:00 AM via its own schedule AND at 2:05 AM triggered by a pipeline, you have a duplicate.

### Step 4: Calculate Duration Regression

Compare each item's P95 execution duration this week versus last month. Items whose P95 has increased by more than 20% are approaching timeout risk and generating increasingly expensive executions.

### Step 5: Estimate CU Waste

For each waste category:

```
Retry Waste = (Failed Runs * Avg Duration * CU Rate)
Duplicate Waste = (Duplicate Runs * Avg Duration * CU Rate)
Duration Regression Waste = (Current P95 - Baseline P95) * Run Count * CU Rate
```

## Five Steps You Can Take Today

You do not need a tool to start reducing CU waste. Here are five actions any Fabric team can implement immediately:

### 1. Set Up Duration Baseline Tracking

Create a Fabric Notebook that runs weekly, queries the Jobs API for every item in your workspace, and writes duration statistics (P50, P95, max) to a Lakehouse table. Compare each week to the baseline. When P95 increases by more than 15%, investigate before it becomes a timeout.

### 2. Audit Your Schedule Overlaps

Query all pipeline triggers and notebook schedules in your workspace. Build a simple time-grid showing what runs when. Look for items that execute more than once in the same window from different trigger sources.

### 3. Implement Incremental Refresh for Large Datasets

If any of your semantic model refreshes take more than 45 minutes, evaluate incremental refresh. The CU savings are dramatic -- often 80-95% for large fact tables. The upfront configuration effort is hours; the ongoing savings are hundreds of CU-hours per month.

### 4. Add Failure Alerting

Fabric does not natively alert you when a pipeline fails. Use Power Automate to poll the Jobs API every 15 minutes and send a Teams notification when a job fails. Faster detection means faster remediation, which means fewer cascading downstream failures.

### 5. Track Your Workspace's "Waste Score"

Define a simple metric:

```
Waste Score = (Failed CU-seconds + Duplicate CU-seconds) / Total CU-seconds * 100
```

A healthy workspace should have a Waste Score below 5%. Above 10% and you are losing real money. Above 20% and you are likely experiencing regular throttling.

## Why This Matters More Than You Think

The CU waste problem is not just about money. It is about capacity reliability.

Every wasted CU-second reduces the headroom available for legitimate workloads. When that headroom shrinks to zero, throttling begins. Background jobs are delayed. Interactive queries slow down. Users notice. And because throttling creates failures, which create retries, which consume more CUs, you get a negative feedback loop that can only be broken by either throwing money at a larger SKU or fixing the root causes.

The teams that win in Fabric are not the ones with the biggest capacity SKUs. They are the ones that understand their CU consumption patterns, catch failures early, eliminate duplicate work, and trend duration metrics before they become timeouts.

That is what observability enables. Not just "what failed?" but "what is this failure costing us, and what else is it affecting?"
